PATH INTEGRALS

Imagine a barrier with two slits in it, as well as a particle traveling towards this barrier, after which comes a particle detector. In the formulation of Quantum Mechanics, we know (and assume) that this particle can be modeled as a wave. So it can travel through either of the slits. To find the probability that a particle will arrive at some arbitrary location xxx﻿, we have to consider both cases.

Now, imagine the number of slits growing larger and larger. The method is still the same. We simply account for all slits as the particle has (possibly different) probabilities of going through each of them.

A (mathematical) plane in empty space is a barrier with infinitely many slits. So we integrate over all of these slits. However, after the first barrier, we can add additional barriers, and empty space can be modeled as infinitely many barriers that each have infinitely many slits.

So the problem becomes integrations over all of space. This is the essence of the Path Integral formulation of Quantum Mechanics.

  • Why Do Classical Particles Only Take One Path?

    After you work out all of the messy integrals, you get an exponential term exp⁡(iS[x(t)]/ℏ)\exp(iS[x(t)]/\hbar)exp(iS[x(t)]/ℏ)﻿ in the resulting integral that "tells" you how the particle will move. Here, x(t)x(t)x(t)﻿ is a path, and SSS﻿ is the classically defined Action (in Lagrangian Mechanics) of the path.

    Now, let us qualitatively reason about the neighbor hood where SSS﻿ is stationary (a.k.a. dSd[x(t)]=0\frac{dS}{d[x(t)]} = 0d[x(t)]dS​=0﻿) with respect to its corresponding path. Let's call this stationary path xox_oxo​﻿. The first order term in the Taylor Expansion of SS S﻿ vanishes, and so the quantity of SSS﻿ will change rather slowly around the path xox_oxo​﻿.

    This enables paths around xox_oxo​﻿ to have a chance at contributing constructively to the integral.

    As one strays further away from xox_oxo​﻿, first order terms become non-negligible, and SSS﻿ starts to change more rapidly. When SSS﻿ is large enough, exp⁡(iS/ℏ)\exp(iS/\hbar)exp(iS/ℏ)﻿ will change very fast. But this is a sinusoidal value! This means that paths will begin to desctructively contribute to the integral.

    This means that non-stationary paths cancel each other out, while paths around the staionary path xox_oxo​﻿ will constribute constructively.

    But this tells us exactly why "classical" (a.k.a. very massive) objects take exactly one predictable path almost all the time! For objects with such large values of SSS﻿, even a slight deviation from the xox_oxo​﻿ will cause the exponential to go crazy.

    This manifests itself as the Principle of Least (Stationary) Action in Classical Mechanics, where objects only take the path with stationary action.

    The Path Integral formalism is computationally daunting and rather impractical. But it offers us a bridge between Quantum and Classical Mechanics.
